Data Consulting

In the Data Consulting committee, members have the opportunity to analyze real-life data, working with companies, nonprofits, and Berkeley-affiliated organizations to advise policy and make data-driven decisions. Students will learn a variety of machine learning techniques and advanced statistical methods, as well as a suite of visualization tools, all in a professional yet instructive setting.

Each semester we work on a diverse slate of projects. From analyzing city traffic data to surveying Cal Undergraduates, our members become experienced data collectors, data mungers, data modelers, data visualizers, and data presenters. We bring skill sets from academia and the workforce to tackle any kind of challenge. More importantly, we come together as a team to learn from each other and collaborate together.

This Semester

Tutorfly - Business and Web Analytics

Spring 2018

Tutorfly is a peer to peer tutoring platform disrupting the 11 billion dollar tutoring space that pairs high school student tutors with younger students in the same community. Parents log on to Tutorfly and can filter by subject, class, and teacher to find a tutor that’s just recently completed the exact same class as their child. Launched in 2017, Tutorfly has completed over 400 sessions to date and is expanding to high schools throughout California. Our data team will perform a set of business analytics tasks that fully address this client’s needs, including setting KPIs, creating a visualization dashboard, analyzing web browsing data, and generating a business report. We also expect to see how our analytics results can actually be reflected through the company’s decisions on newly launched projects.

UIzard - Automating UI Code

Spring 2018

Our team will be focused on rapid implementation of deep learning papers, culminating in a final project with Danish startup UIzard. Led by Tony Beltramelli, this startup pioneered one of the first papers on turning sketches of User Interfaces for iOS and web apps into their respective code (Swift, React).

Before we work with UIzard, we’ll be implementing 1-2 deep learning papers to sharpen our skills. The implemented papers or relevant engineering feats will be submitted to a relevant open source deep learning codebase. We’ll be working heavily with AWS, Python, and Unix.

Data for Good Competition (Housing Crisis) - Data visualizations

Spring 2018

This team will be focusing on the Data for Good competition, by investigating the housing situation in California. Our project team will be competing in this project sponsored by Facebook, for social good efforts. We will create data visualizations describing the housing situation, such as heatmaps of salary, housing costs, and availability. An end term goal may be to create matching tools for citizens to input factors such as salary, and employer location, and suggest viable locations to look for housing.

Past Projects

Population Modeling

Fall 2017

Population modeling is crucial for overall growth as it determines resource allocation and future plans. However, population modeling can be extremely challenging due to hundreds of time-sensitive variables. For the fall 2017 semester, the population modeling team worked to create models for population rates based on four elementary features: birth rate, mortality rate, fertility rate, and life expectancy. Using machine learning and time series techniques, we compared our models to the UN model as a standard. Click to see our blog post and our approximations!

Facial Emotion Recognition

Fall 2017

Deep learning is part of the broader machine learning context that learns data representations using complex architectures. The team worked on building a state of the art deep learning model that classifies human facial images into 7 different emotions: happy, sad, angry, fear, neutral, surprise and disgust. Different models, from CNN to Ensemble Learning, were evaluated and compared based on their performance.

Food Insecurity

Fall 2017

The main objectives for this project was to identify areas of Alameda County in need of greater food assistance. We conducted literature review and exploratory data analysis on various social, economic and demographic features of Alameda County to inform our model and feature selection. We then created a predictive model to predict pounds unmet by census tract.

Electrical Power

Fall 2016

The electric grid underlying American energy production and usage is a highly complex system, with many dynamic parts and facets. In the Fall 2016 semester, SUSA was hired by the environmental policy start-up, Environmental Progress, to model the relationships between the productions of power plants of differing fuel types on the Californian grid over time, in the context to the imminent closure of the Diablo Canyon Power Plant, the last remaining nuclear power plant in California. We extrapolated from the production data of the grid to predict the environmental effects of such a closure, and even extended our analysis to gauge the health effects the plant's shutdown could have on surrounding populations.

Crime in Berkeley

Spring 2016

A common concern for Berkeley students is the relatively concerning amount of crime in surrounding areas. For the Spring 2016 semester, we plan to investigate crime rate in Berkeley and surrounding areas. We hope to create a basic predictive model that will help Berkeley students decide whether walking home is the best decision.

Fires in San Francisco

Spring 2016

Residents of the Mission District of San Francisco have been expressing concern over an apparently increasing rate of fires happening in their neighborhood. For the Spring 2016 semester, we plan to analyze if there is indeed an increasing amount of fires happening in the Mission District, and if so, we will investigate why this trend is occurring.
